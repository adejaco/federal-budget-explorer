G7 International Comparisons

How the U.S. Compares

Fiscal and spending comparisons across OECD nations. Data sourced from OECD, IMF, and NATO — all on a consistent all-government basis. Select a region group to change which countries appear on all charts.

Region / Country Group — USA always shown as anchor
Total Tax Revenue as % of GDP (2015–2024)
All government levels including social contributions. Source: OECD Revenue Statistics 2025, Table 3.1.
Note: U.S. domestic figures often cite federal-only (~17%), which is not comparable to other countries' all-government totals. This chart uses all-government totals for all countries. Norway: tax revenue elevated by petroleum sector; Chile: revenue sensitive to copper prices — fluctuations partly reflect commodity cycles rather than policy changes.
Annual Fiscal Deficit as % of GDP (2015–2024)
General government balance. Y-axis inverted: larger deficits appear at top. Source: IMF WEO October 2024.
Note: U.S. 2023 deficit was partly inflated by a student loan accounting adjustment (~1.3pp). Germany ran surpluses 2015–2019 before COVID. Norway: surpluses almost entirely driven by petroleum revenues and sovereign wealth fund returns — the underlying non-oil economy runs a structural deficit of ~9–10% of mainland GDP. The 2022 spike (+25.5%) reflects the Ukraine war energy price surge. Israel: swung from a +0.6% surplus in 2022 to −4.2% in 2023 and −6.8% in 2024 due to wartime spending following October 7, 2023.
Total Government Debt (2015–2024)
Gross government debt. Source: IMF WEO October 2024.
Note: Japan's gross debt (~255% GDP) significantly overstates net obligations; Japan holds large financial assets, making net debt ~155% GDP. U.S. nominal debt ($35.7T in 2024) exceeds the other six G7 members combined. Norway: gross debt (~55% GDP) is largely offset by the Government Pension Fund Global (GPFG), the world's largest sovereign wealth fund at ~$2.1T (~400% of mainland GDP) — Norway is a large net creditor nation despite the gross debt figure.
Defense Spending as % of GDP (2015–2025)
Source: NATO June 2025 estimates; SIPRI for Japan (not a NATO member).
Note: U.S. at 3.4% is roughly double the 2% NATO target. NATO's new target is 3.5% core defense by 2035. Sweden and Denmark have both committed to exceed 3% by 2026. Israel: defense spending surged from 5.2% of GDP in 2023 to 8.4–8.8% in 2024–25 due to the Gaza war and multi-front conflict — this is wartime mobilization, not a structural baseline. Pre-war (2022) level was ~4.5%.
Total Health Spending as % of GDP (2015–2023)
Total health expenditure (public + private). Source: OECD Health at a Glance 2025.
Note: U.S. at 17.2% is nearly double Germany (12.3%), yet the U.S. has the lowest G7 life expectancy (78.4 yrs) and ~27.6M uninsured. Public share varies widely: UK ~81%, France ~77%, USA ~48%.
Public Education Spending as % of GDP (2015–2022)
Government education expenditure all levels. Source: OECD Education at a Glance 2025.
Note: G7 clustered at 3.8–5.8%. U.S. figure is slightly inflated by university R&D. Japan's low public figure is offset by high private education spending.
Total R&D Spending (GERD) as % of GDP (2015–2023)
Gross expenditure on R&D, public + private combined. Source: OECD MSTI March 2025.
Note: U.S. leads G7 at 3.6%. UK at 1.9% is frequently cited as a structural weakness. Italy and Canada fall below the OECD average of 2.7%.
GDP per Capita, PPP (2015–2024)
Current international dollars (PPP-adjusted). Source: IMF WEO April 2026 / World Bank WDI.
Note: PPP adjusts for price level differences across countries, giving a better measure of living standards than nominal GDP. Norway and Switzerland are elevated partly by oil wealth and financial sector wages respectively.
Real GDP Growth Rate (2015–2024)
Annual % change, inflation-adjusted. Source: IMF WEO April 2026.
Note: 2020 contraction visible across all countries (COVID). Ireland's 2015 figure (+25%) reflects multinational IP transfers and is excluded as a distortion. Costa Rica, South Korea, and Israel show the strongest long-run growth among peers.
Unemployment Rate (2015–2024)
Standardized unemployment rate, % of labor force. Source: OECD Labour Statistics 2025.
Note: OECD standardized rates are comparable across countries. Colombia's headline rate understates true underemployment given high informality (~60% of workforce). Italy's high structural unemployment reflects regional north-south divergence.
Inflation Rate, CPI (2015–2024)
Annual % change in consumer prices. Source: IMF WEO April 2026 / OECD CPI Statistics.
Note: 2021–2023 inflation spike was global, driven by supply chain disruptions and energy prices post-COVID. Most OECD countries have returned to near-target rates by 2024. Switzerland maintained the lowest inflation throughout.

Comparability Note — Tax Revenue
U.S. domestic sources typically report federal revenue only (~17% of GDP). This is not comparable to other countries' all-government totals. The INTL Comps page uses OECD all-government totals for all countries. The two figures should not be combined arithmetically.
Payroll Tax Coverage
All payroll tax figures include both the employee and employer share of FICA contributions (Social Security: 6.2% each = 12.4% combined; Medicare: 1.45% each = 2.9% combined). Employers remit both shares to the IRS.
Fiscal Year Convention
The U.S. federal fiscal year runs October 1 – September 30. A new president inherits ~9 months of the prior administration's budget framework in their first year.
Projection Methodology
All projections use CBO's February 2026 baseline, incorporating enacted legislation including the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (signed July 2025). Projections are baselines, not forecasts.
Rounding
All figures rounded to nearest billion dollars. Subtotals may not add to totals due to rounding.
